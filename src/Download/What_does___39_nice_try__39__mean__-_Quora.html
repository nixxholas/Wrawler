What does "nice try" mean?

3 Answers

Robert Charles Lee, 35+ years in editorial & publishing, British speaker, work in American English
1.6k Views · Most Viewed Writer in English (language) with 3900+ answers
"Nice try" (especially when used on its own in speech) is in the sense of "good effort, but not believable because I'm no fool." In other words, what that person said is farfetched enough that only an idiot would believe. This dialogue should show a sense of that:

A: I'm a test pilot.
B: Nice try. D'you take me for a fool?
A: I can please you like you've never been pleased before.
B: Nice try too. Then please me: please leave me. *wink*

(From an actual conversation. The look on A's face was priceless.)

The primary use of "nice try" (British, I think) was a polite way of saying, "You didn't get it right, but made an effort. You should try again."

The secondary usage has come to be an ironic (or even sarcastic) inversion:  "Well, look at you. You failed."

Teachers often use the first type, jerks the second. In present-day U.S., the second generally prevails. I blame "Saturday Night Live."
