    It means it felt like a very long day, you can have an 8 hour day that goes very quickly, and someone else, let's say someone doing a lot of work involving lifting and moving etc, if the day feels like it has actually been 100 hours, he might say "I've had a long day"

    It has nothing to do with the actual length of time, just how the time felt to him.
    If he is listening to music one day, and works 8 hours, he might not say it, let's say, the next day, he didn't feel well, he had no music to listen to, he wanted to go home because it was hungry... or the day felt like it was going very slowly, this means he is more likely to say "I've had a long day".

  5. Thomas Tompion Senior Member

    This is a common device in English: the transferred epithet. The day is not any longer than usual but you transfer your feelings to the day itself, as people have explained. Similarly we talk of having a hard day: it doesn't mean it's made of concrete, or an animated meal, which doesn't mean that the steaks were leaping around and talking to each other.

  7. Dimcl Senior Member

    British Columbia, Canada
    Canadian English
    Maybe yes, maybe no. "I had a long day today" is pretty-much a set phrase that is used to describe what the previous posters have explained.

    If this guy really loves his job and put in 12 hours today instead of his usual eight, it may not have seemed like a long day. On the other hand, if he's very tired and didn't want to put in the extra hours but had no choice, his attitude wouldn't be very good and the time would have seemed to drag. In this event, he might say it.

    The other thing is that if you put in many hours, you will become more and more tired. After 12 hours on the job when you're not used to it, you will be extra-tired when you arrive home. Just being very tired can cause you to use this phrase.

  10. JenniferOverington Member

    English - Canada
    Yes to the above. It's been a long day / I've had a long day... both refer to feelings which may be due to tedium, boredom, or working hard. But usually people who work are in their work so actually the day goes by fast. Usually tedium makes the day seem longer. It can also apply to literally longer hours, or simply more aggravating stress and waiting around for absolutely nothing, as in: "Nothing happened at work. I've had a long day. I'm so sick and tired of being there."

    I've often questioned the notion of "hard work" - it seems an oxymoron. Either there's work to do or there isn't. When there is work to do the day goes by fast and is fun. When there is nothing to do that's "having a long day". Other opinions?

    For example, right now, this forum is making me wait and re-post for no reason, citing the bogus reason that I posted something else 30 seconds ago which I didn't. That's an example of having a long day.
